üéØ General Photography Guidelines

Lighting

‚úì DO

  • Use natural daylight when possible
  • Take photos in the morning (8-10 AM) or late afternoon (3-5 PM)
  • Ensure even lighting across the surface
  • Use flash in dark areas, but avoid harsh shadows

‚úó DON'T

  • Take photos in direct harsh sunlight at noon
  • Shoot against bright light (backlit)
  • Take photos in heavy shadows
  • Use colored lighting that distorts material colors

Distance & Framing

Optimal Distance: Stand 3-5 feet (1-1.5 meters) from the subject for most materials. Get closer for detailed defects, further back for overall quality.

‚úì DO

  • Fill the frame with the construction element
  • Capture at least 1-2 square feet of the surface
  • Include a reference object (ruler, coin) for scale if possible
  • Keep the camera parallel to the surface

‚úó DON'T

  • Take photos from extreme angles
  • Zoom in so much that context is lost
  • Include too much unnecessary background
  • Take blurry or shaky photos

Image Quality

  • Resolution: Use at least 2MP camera quality (1920x1080 or higher)
  • Focus: Tap the screen to focus on the construction element before shooting
  • Stability: Hold the phone steady or use a tripod for critical inspections
  • Format: JPEG or PNG formats work best

üèóÔ∏è Category-Specific Photo Tips

üß±Mortar/Concrete Mix

  • What to capture: Fresh or recent mix showing consistency and color
  • Distance: 2-3 feet to show mix uniformity
  • Angle: Slightly overhead view (45-60 degrees)
  • Focus on: Water-cement ratio, aggregate distribution, color uniformity
  • Include: Multiple areas of the same batch if possible

üèóÔ∏èFlooring/Pavement (Tiles, Concrete Floors)

  • What to capture: Large section showing levelness and finish
  • Distance: 4-6 feet to capture 6-8 square feet of surface
  • Angle: Low angle to show levelness, straight-on for finish quality
  • Focus on: Joint alignment, levelness, cracks, tile placement
  • Include: Reference line or level tool in frame if checking levelness

ü™µWood/Timber

  • What to capture: Length of timber showing grain and any defects
  • Distance: Capture at least 3-4 feet of length
  • Angle: Straight-on to show grain pattern clearly
  • Focus on: Knots, cracks, warping, moisture signs, insect damage
  • Include: End grain photo for seasoning assessment

üé®Painted Surface

  • What to capture: Finished painted area showing texture and coverage
  • Distance: 3-4 feet for overall quality, closer for defects
  • Angle: Slight angle (30 degrees) to show surface texture and finish
  • Focus on: Paint evenness, brush marks, drips, coverage, color uniformity
  • Include: Areas with and without direct light to show finish quality

üß±Exposed Brick Wall (Before Plaster)

  • What to capture: Brickwork showing coursing and mortar joints
  • Distance: 3-5 feet to capture at least 20-30 bricks
  • Angle: Straight-on perpendicular view
  • Focus on: Brick alignment, joint thickness, mortar quality, levelness
  • Include: Corner or edge view if checking plumbness

üî©Steel/Rebar

  • What to capture: Rebar layout showing spacing and condition
  • Distance: 4-6 feet to show spacing pattern
  • Angle: Top-down view for spacing, close-up for corrosion
  • Focus on: Rust/corrosion, diameter verification, spacing, cover depth
  • Include: Measuring tape or ruler for spacing verification

ü™®Stone

  • What to capture: Stone surface or cut showing type and quality
  • Distance: 2-4 feet depending on stone size
  • Angle: Straight-on for surface finish, multiple angles for cracks
  • Focus on: Stone type, cracks, finish quality, weathering, color
  • Include: Natural light to show true color and texture

‚è≥Sand / üèñÔ∏èM-Sand

  • What to capture: Sand sample spread on flat surface
  • Distance: 1-2 feet close-up view
  • Angle: Top-down overhead view
  • Focus on: Grain size distribution, silt content, color, uniformity
  • Include: Spread sand on white paper or plate for contrast
  • Tip: Take photo of dry sample for accurate color assessment

üß¥Plaster Quality (Before Paint)

  • What to capture: Plastered wall showing finish and evenness
  • Distance: 4-5 feet for overall quality, closer for cracks
  • Angle: Low angle (grazing light) to show unevenness
  • Focus on: Surface evenness, cracks, damp patches, bonding with substrate
  • Include: Photo in angled light to reveal surface irregularities

üíßWaterproofing

  • What to capture: Waterproofing membrane or treatment area
  • Distance: 3-5 feet to show coverage area
  • Angle: Straight-on for application quality, overhead for ponding test
  • Focus on: Membrane integrity, overlap seams, corner detailing, coverage
  • Include: Close-ups of critical areas like corners, joints, penetrations

üí¶Concrete Curing

  • What to capture: Curing method and concrete surface condition
  • Distance: 3-6 feet to show curing coverage
  • Angle: Overhead or slight angle to show moisture retention
  • Focus on: Wet/dry areas, curing method (water, membrane, etc.), coverage
  • Include: Time stamp or note on concrete age if possible

üìêShuttering/Formwork

  • What to capture: Formwork assembly showing alignment and joints
  • Distance: 5-8 feet to show overall structure
  • Angle: Multiple angles - front, corner, top view
  • Focus on: Alignment, tightness of joints, oil application, support structure
  • Include: Close-ups of joint areas and corner details

üîóJoint Work

  • What to capture: Expansion or construction joints along their length
  • Distance: 2-3 feet to show joint detail
  • Angle: Straight-on perpendicular to joint line
  • Focus on: Joint placement, width, sealing material, continuity, cleanliness
  • Include: Several feet of joint length to assess consistency

‚ö°Electrical/Plumbing Rough-in

  • What to capture: Conduit or pipe installation before closing
  • Distance: 4-6 feet to show routing and layout
  • Angle: Straight-on to show depth and alignment
  • Focus on: Conduit placement, pipe slopes, fixing method, spacing
  • Include: Close-ups of connections, bends, and support points

‚ùå Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Dirty lens: Clean your camera lens before taking photos
  • Too far away: AI cannot analyze details from distant photos
  • Poor lighting: Dark or overly bright photos reduce accuracy
  • Motion blur: Keep camera steady while shooting
  • Obstructions: Remove tools, materials, or people from frame
  • Wrong angle: Extreme angles distort true condition
  • Mixed categories: Focus on one category per photo
  • Wet surfaces: Water can hide defects; photograph when dry (except for curing)

üí° Pro Tips for Best Results

Multiple Photos: Take 2-3 photos of the same element from different angles for comprehensive analysis.
Context Matters: Include enough surrounding area to understand the installation context.
Timing: Photograph critical items BEFORE they get covered (rebar before concrete, plaster before paint).
Document Progress: Take photos at different stages to track quality over time.
Scale Reference: Include a measuring tape, ruler, or coin for size reference when assessing dimensions.
Clean the Surface: Remove dust or debris before photographing to see the true condition.
